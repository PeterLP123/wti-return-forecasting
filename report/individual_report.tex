Our earlier analysis suggested that the LSTM's learned $\sigma_t$ is ``calibrated and regime-aware,'' but that interpretation was not tested with formal statistical evidence.
This study applies a battery of goodness-of-fit tests to the standardised residuals of two LSTM variants and a GARCH(1,1) benchmark, finding that the pure-NLL LSTM produces partially calibrated uncertainty estimates while the composite decision-aware loss materially distorts $\sigma_t$.

\end{quote}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}
%% =========================================================================

Forecasting next-day returns in commodity markets is a task characterised by extremely low signal-to-noise ratios.
When models are trained under standard loss functions such as mean squared error or Gaussian negative log-likelihood (NLL), the optimal prediction is typically close to zero, the unconditional mean of the return distribution.
This phenomenon, referred to in the earlier analysis as the ``flat-line problem,'' is not a failure of modelling but a consequence of the signal structure: the conditional mean of daily log returns is very close to zero, and any aggressive departure risks large quadratic penalties.

To address this problem, the earlier analysis introduced a composite three-component loss function that combines Gaussian NLL with a directional penalty and a variance-collapse penalty.
Under this composite loss, the LSTM produced non-trivial directional predictions (52.3\% accuracy versus 47.1\% under pure NLL) and a ten-fold increase in prediction spread.
That earlier analysis also interpreted the LSTM's learned $\sigma_t$ as ``calibrated and regime-aware,'' based on two pieces of informal evidence: the unconditional average of $\sigma_t$ ($\approx 0.025$) is close to the empirical return standard deviation ($\approx 0.023$), and the uncertainty bands visually widen during volatile periods.

Neither of these observations constitutes a formal calibration test.
Matching unconditional moments is a necessary but not sufficient condition for probabilistic calibration; visual band-widening is not quantitative.
Moreover, the composite loss is explicitly not a proper scoring rule in the sense of \citet{gneiting2007strictly}.
To see why, note that a scoring rule $S(F, r)$ is \emph{proper} if its expectation is minimised when the forecaster reports the true predictive distribution $G$: $\mathbb{E}_{r \sim G}[S(G, r)] \leq \mathbb{E}_{r \sim G}[S(F, r)]$ for all $F$.
The Gaussian NLL satisfies this property because it is the logarithmic score for the Gaussian family.
But the composite loss adds terms --- $\mathcal{L}_{\text{dir}}$ and $\mathcal{L}_{\text{var}}$ - that depend on $\mu_t$ through non-probabilistic objectives.
The expectation of these terms under the true distribution is not minimised at $F = G$, so the composite loss cannot be proper: the model is incentivised to report a distribution different from its best estimate of the truth, and $\sigma_t$ absorbs the resulting distortion.

Calibration of neural network uncertainty estimates is a known challenge in the broader deep learning literature \citep{lakshminarayanan2017simple,kuleshov2018accurate}, and the specific question of whether heteroscedastic regression networks produce calibrated variance estimates has received less attention in the financial forecasting context.
It is also natural to consider using $\sigma_t$ for position sizing in a simulated trading strategy.
Before $\sigma_t$ can be trusted for downstream risk management, its calibration must be established - or its miscalibration must be understood.

This report addresses this gap by conducting a focused post-hoc calibration study of the models developed in the earlier analysis.
The central research question is:

\begin{quote}
\emph{Are the uncertainty estimates produced by our LSTM models statistically calibrated, and how do they compare with a classical GARCH benchmark?}
\end{quote}

Three hypotheses guide the analysis:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item the pure-NLL LSTM should be better calibrated than the composite decision-aware model because NLL is a proper scoring rule;
    \item both models are likely to exhibit heavy-tailed residuals relative to the Gaussian assumption, so a Student-$t$ predictive law should improve fit;
    \item the LSTM's volatility estimates may not outperform a GARCH(1,1) benchmark once evaluated using formal density-forecast diagnostics.
\end{enumerate}

The contribution is not to propose a new forecasting model, but to extend the earlier analysis using established tools from forecast evaluation and volatility modelling.
By doing so, the report complements the earlier emphasis on directional prediction and demonstrates why uncertainty calibration must be judged by proper statistical tests rather than visual plausibility alone.

\section{Data Analysis}

\subsection{Dataset and target variable}

The analysis uses the dataset prepared for the earlier study: daily observations for the \textbf{WTI crude oil front-month futures contract} from January 2010 to December 2024.
The target variable is the one-day-ahead log return,
\begin{equation}
    r_t = \log P_t - \log P_{t-1},
\end{equation}
where $P_t$ denotes the daily settlement price.
The 51 predictors used by the LSTM comprise lagged returns, rolling volatility measures, volume/open-interest features, market microstructure variables, and calendar indicators.
Because the predictive mean of $r_t$ is economically small, the main object of interest for this report is not $\mu_t$ itself but the accompanying volatility estimate $\sigma_t$.

\subsection{Train/validation/test split}

To preserve temporal ordering and avoid look-ahead bias, the study uses a walk-forward split:
\begin{itemize}
    \item training: January 2010 -- December 2022;
    \item validation: January 2023 -- December 2023;
    \item test: January 2024 -- December 2024.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Models under study}

The report evaluates the two LSTM variants developed in the earlier analysis:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Pure-NLL LSTM.}
    This model outputs $(\mu_t, \sigma_t)$ and is trained by minimising the Gaussian negative log-likelihood,
    \begin{equation}
        \mathcal{L}_{\text{NLL}} = \frac{1}{2}\log(2\pi\sigma_t^2) + \frac{(r_t-\mu_t)^2}{2\sigma_t^2},
        \label{eq:nll}
    \end{equation}
    which is a proper scoring rule and therefore incentivises honest distributional forecasts.

    \item \textbf{Composite-loss LSTM.}
    This model augments NLL with directional and variance-spread penalties,
    \begin{equation}
        \mathcal{L}_{\text{comp}} = \mathcal{L}_{\text{NLL}} + \lambda_1\mathcal{L}_{\text{dir}} + \lambda_2\mathcal{L}_{\text{var}},
        \label{eq:comp}
    \end{equation}
    where $\mathcal{L}_{\text{dir}}$ rewards correct directional calls and $\mathcal{L}_{\text{var}}$ discourages collapse to an unconditional variance forecast.
    This objective aims to improve trading usefulness, but it sacrifices the strict probabilistic interpretation of $\sigma_t$.
\end{enumerate}

In addition, a \textbf{GARCH(1,1)} model is fit to the training returns as a classical volatility benchmark.
GARCH is the standard tool for modelling conditional heteroscedasticity in financial returns, building on Engle's original ARCH framework \citep{engle1982autoregressive,bollerslev1986generalized}, and provides a natural reference point.
The earlier modelling setup also connects the LSTM's heteroscedastic output to GARCH-style volatility modelling, making this comparison directly relevant.

Figure~\ref{fig:eda} presents the test-set return distribution alongside fitted Gaussian and Student-$t$ density overlays, a Q-Q plot, a P-P plot, and the rolling 60-day realised volatility.
The Q-Q plot reveals clear tail departures from the Gaussian model, particularly in the left tail.
The rolling volatility panel shows distinct volatility regimes during the test period, providing the visual context for assessing whether the models' $\sigma_t$ tracks these regimes.

\section{Methodology}
\label{sec:methodology}
%% =========================================================================

\subsection{Probabilistic calibration}

A probabilistic forecaster that outputs $(\mu_t, \sigma_t)$ is \emph{calibrated} if, for every nominal coverage level $\alpha \in (0,1)$, the empirical fraction of observations falling within the $\alpha$-prediction interval equals $\alpha$:
\begin{equation}
    \frac{1}{n}\sum_{t=1}^{n} \mathbf{1}\!\left[r_t \in \left(\mu_t - q_{\alpha}\,\sigma_t,\;\mu_t + q_{\alpha}\,\sigma_t\right)\right] \approx \alpha,
    \label{eq:coverage}
\end{equation}
where $q_{\alpha}$ is the $(1+\alpha)/2$ quantile of the assumed predictive distribution.

An equivalent characterisation is provided by the probability integral transform (PIT).
If the predictive distribution $F_t(r) = \Phi\!\left((r - \mu_t)/\sigma_t\right)$ is correctly specified, the PIT values
\begin{equation}
    u_t = \Phi\!\left(\frac{r_t - \mu_t}{\sigma_t}\right)
    \label{eq:pit}
\end{equation}
should be i.i.d. Uniform$(0,1)$ \citep{diebold1998evaluating}.
Thus, calibration can be assessed through several complementary diagnostics:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{distributional fit}: are the standardised residuals $z_t = (r_t-\mu_t)/\sigma_t$ approximately mean-zero and variance-one under the assumed law?
    \item \textbf{independence}: are $z_t$ and $|z_t|$ free from serial dependence?
    \item \textbf{coverage}: do nominal prediction intervals attain their target frequencies?
    \item \textbf{PIT uniformity}: are PIT values close to uniform, without U-shapes or inverse-U patterns?
\end{itemize}

No single test is decisive in isolation.
A model may pass moment matching while failing tail calibration, or achieve correct unconditional coverage while remaining conditionally miscalibrated during volatile regimes.
For that reason, the report uses a battery of diagnostics rather than one summary metric.

\subsection{Statistical tests}

The core analysis is based on the standardised residuals
\begin{equation}
    z_t = \frac{r_t - \mu_t}{\sigma_t}.
    \label{eq:z}
\end{equation}
If the Gaussian predictive distribution is well calibrated, then ideally $z_t \overset{i.i.d.}{\sim} \mathcal{N}(0,1)$.
Four classes of tests are applied.

\paragraph{1. Moment and shape diagnostics.}
Sample mean, standard deviation, skewness, and excess kurtosis provide a first-pass assessment.
For a calibrated Gaussian density, these should be close to $(0,1,0,0)$.
Large deviations immediately indicate bias, under-/over-dispersion, or misspecified tails.

\paragraph{2. Independence diagnostics.}
Autocorrelation functions of $z_t$ and $|z_t|$ are examined visually, and the Ljung--Box test is applied at lag 10 \citep{ljung1978measure}.
Serial correlation in $z_t$ implies mean misspecification; serial correlation in $|z_t|$ implies unmodelled volatility persistence.
Either failure indicates that the predictive density has not captured the time-series structure of returns.

\paragraph{3. Distributional goodness-of-fit.}
The Kolmogorov--Smirnov (KS) test compares the empirical CDF of $z_t$ with the standard normal CDF \citep{kolmogorov1933sulla}, while the Anderson--Darling (AD) test emphasises tail discrepancies \citep{anderson1952asymptotic}.
These tests complement moment diagnostics by formalising whether the entire shape of the predictive law is plausible.

\paragraph{4. Interval and PIT diagnostics.}
For nominal levels from 50\% to 99\%, the empirical coverage of Gaussian prediction intervals is compared with the target coverage.
The PIT histogram is also examined: a U-shape indicates under-dispersion, an inverse-U indicates over-dispersion, and skewness indicates location bias \citep{diebold1998evaluating}.

\subsection{GARCH(1,1) benchmark}

To benchmark the LSTM's uncertainty estimates against a classical volatility model, the report fits a Gaussian GARCH(1,1) to the training returns,
\begin{align}
    r_t &= \varepsilon_t, \qquad \varepsilon_t \mid \mathcal{F}_{t-1} \sim \mathcal{N}(0,h_t), \\
    h_t &= \omega + \alpha\varepsilon_{t-1}^2 + \beta h_{t-1},
    \label{eq:garch}
\end{align}
where $h_t$ is the conditional variance.
The benchmark is intentionally simple: it uses no exogenous features and a zero conditional mean, but it is designed specifically to model volatility clustering.
If the deep model's $\sigma_t$ cannot outperform GARCH on calibration grounds, then its additional complexity has little value for probabilistic forecasting.

Out-of-sample forecasts for the test period are produced by recursively applying Equation~\eqref{eq:garch}, bridging through the validation period to preserve the volatility state.

\subsection{Conditional calibration}

Unconditional calibration tests may mask regime-dependent miscalibration.
If a model is overconfident during volatile periods and overdispersed during calm periods, the aggregate statistics may average to acceptable values.
To detect such behaviour, the test set is split into high-volatility and low-volatility subsets using the median of the rolling 60-day realised volatility as the threshold, following the conditional calibration framework of \citet{kuleshov2018accurate}.
The calibration battery is then applied separately to each subset.

\subsection{\texorpdfstring{Student-$t$ extension (Chapter 14)}{Student-t extension (Chapter 14)}}

After documenting Gaussian miscalibration, the analysis relaxes the distributional assumption.
For each model's standardised residuals $z_t$, the degrees-of-freedom parameter $\hat{\nu}$ is estimated by maximum likelihood, maximising
\begin{equation}
    \ell(\nu) = \sum_{t=1}^{n}\log f_{\nu}(z_t),
    \label{eq:tlik}
\end{equation}
where $f_{\nu}$ is the density of a standardised Student-$t$ variable with variance one.
The fit is restricted to $\nu > 2$ so that the variance exists.
If the heavy tails of returns are the main source of Gaussian miscalibration, then replacing $\mathcal{N}(0,1)$ with $t_{\nu}(0,1)$ should improve the Q-Q alignment and reduce the rejection rate of the goodness-of-fit tests.

\subsection{Limitations}

Several limitations should be noted.
First, only one test year is available for evaluation, so tail-focused tests have modest power.
Second, the report treats the LSTM forecasts as fixed and does not retrain or re-specify the underlying models.
Third, calibration is examined for one-step-ahead daily returns only; intraday or multi-horizon forecasts may behave differently.
Finally, because the composite specification was optimised partly for directional utility rather than purely for density accuracy, poor calibration of the composite model is not necessarily a modelling mistake --- but it does mean that its $\sigma_t$ should not be read as a well-calibrated probabilistic uncertainty estimate.

\subsection{GARCH(1,1) benchmark}

The GARCH results sharpen the interpretation of the LSTM findings.
The NLL LSTM does not dominate GARCH in any decisive way on the calibration metrics.
This matters because the LSTM is much richer in inputs and functional flexibility.
If that additional complexity does not improve the quality of the predictive density, then its practical advantage lies elsewhere --- perhaps in directional forecasting, but not in uncertainty quantification.

The most principled summary of probabilistic performance is the average out-of-sample log score (equivalently, negative log-likelihood).

A Diebold--Mariano test \citep{diebold1995comparing} on the daily log-score differentials reveals that the NLL LSTM significantly outperforms the composite LSTM ($\bar{d} = +0.137$ nats/day, $t = 4.65$, $p < 0.001$), confirming that the composite loss degrades probabilistic quality in a statistically robust sense.
In contrast, the NLL LSTM does \emph{not} significantly outperform GARCH ($\bar{d} = +0.023$ nats/day, $t = 0.43$, $p = 0.67$).
This is a striking result: despite conditioning on 51 features through a deep recurrent architecture, the NLL LSTM's predictive density is statistically indistinguishable from a three-parameter GARCH model that uses only lagged returns.
The GARCH-versus-composite comparison is borderline ($p = 0.087$), suggesting that even GARCH tends to outperform the composite LSTM on probabilistic grounds.

To quantify the practical magnitude of these differences, the mean daily log-score gap between the NLL LSTM and the composite model is $0.197$ bits per day.
Over the 348-day test period, this corresponds to a cumulative log-likelihood ratio of $95.1$, which is overwhelmingly significant.
For the NLL LSTM versus GARCH, the daily gap is only $0.032$ bits per day --- a negligible difference relative to the daily volatility of the log-score differential.

This confirms that part of the observed miscalibration came from using a light-tailed predictive family rather than from volatility-scale errors alone.

\subsection{Christoffersen conditional coverage test}

To supplement the simple coverage counts, the 95\% intervals are also evaluated with the Christoffersen conditional coverage framework \citep{christoffersen1998evaluating}, which jointly tests whether exceedances occur with the correct frequency and are serially independent.
At the 95\% level, the NLL LSTM and GARCH both pass the unconditional and independence components, while the composite model fails the unconditional coverage component due to its chronic over-coverage.
This corroborates the earlier rolling-window evidence that the composite model's intervals are not merely conservative on average but systematically mis-scaled.

\subsection{Position-sizing backtest}

Although this report focuses on calibration rather than trading performance, a brief position-sizing illustration helps explain the economic importance of uncertainty quality.
Suppose daily position size is scaled inversely with the predicted standard deviation, $w_t \propto 1/\sigma_t$.
If $\sigma_t$ is overestimated, the strategy becomes too conservative and leaves return on the table; if it is underestimated, realised risk exceeds target.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}
%% =========================================================================

\subsection{Assessment of hypotheses}

The results support all three hypotheses stated in the introduction.

\paragraph{Hypothesis 1: The pure-NLL LSTM is better calibrated than the composite-loss model.}
Strongly supported.
The NLL model outperforms the composite model on residual variance, coverage, PIT shape, KS/AD tests, and out-of-sample log score.
This is exactly what one would expect from the theory of proper scoring rules: when a model is trained to maximise density accuracy, its uncertainty estimates retain statistical meaning; when additional utility-driven penalties are added, that meaning is diluted.

\paragraph{Hypothesis 2: Heavy tails matter, and a Student-$t$ law improves fit.}
Supported.
For every model, moving from Gaussian to Student-$t$ predictive assumptions improves Q-Q alignment and lowers the average NLL.
The improvement is especially important in the tails, where Gaussian predictive intervals are too light.
This finding is consistent with the well-known excess-kurtosis properties of commodity returns \citep{cont2001empirical}.

\paragraph{Hypothesis 3: The LSTM may not outperform GARCH in volatility calibration.}
Also supported.
The NLL LSTM is statistically indistinguishable from GARCH in density-forecast performance, and GARCH marginally dominates on several calibration diagnostics.
This does not imply that deep learning is useless in finance; rather, it shows that richer nonlinear models do not automatically yield better probabilistic forecasts, particularly when the target is noisy and the evaluation horizon is short.

\subsection{Comparison with GARCH(1,1)}

The GARCH comparison is methodologically important.
Deep sequence models are often justified by their capacity to exploit nonlinear dependence across many features, but volatility forecasting has a very strong classical baseline.
When the objective is density calibration rather than directional accuracy, parsimonious time-series models remain difficult to beat.
This result echoes the broader empirical literature, which frequently finds that GARCH(1,1) is a surprisingly resilient volatility benchmark \citep{hansen2005forecast}.

\subsection{Implications for position sizing}

Using $\sigma_t$ to size positions should therefore be interpreted cautiously.
The NLL model's volatility estimates may be usable with further calibration adjustments or a heavier-tailed predictive family.
The composite model's estimates, however, would embed a systematic risk-allocation error because they overstate uncertainty in calm periods and leave residual structure unmodelled.
A trading system built on those raw $\sigma_t$ outputs would likely under-utilise capital while still failing to control tail risk properly.

\subsection{Limitations}

Several caveats qualify the conclusions.
First, the report evaluates only one market (WTI crude oil) and one forecast horizon (one day).
Second, the analysis is ex post and based on exported model forecasts rather than end-to-end retraining.
Third, some diagnostics --- especially goodness-of-fit tests in the tails --- have limited power in a single year of daily data.
Finally, the report does not evaluate whether post-hoc recalibration methods such as isotonic regression on predictive intervals \citep{kuleshov2018accurate} could repair the composite model sufficiently for practical use.

%% =========================================================================
\section{Conclusion and Future Work}
\label{sec:conclusion}
%% =========================================================================

The main conclusion is clear: the uncertainty estimates from the pure-NLL LSTM are only partially calibrated, whereas those from the composite-loss LSTM are not calibrated in a probabilistic sense.
The composite objective improves directional forecasting but materially degrades the meaning of $\sigma_t$ as an estimator of predictive uncertainty.
A simple GARCH(1,1) benchmark performs at least as well as the NLL LSTM on most calibration diagnostics and slightly better on some.

This outcome sharpens the interpretation of our earlier findings.
The LSTM's volatility output may still be \emph{useful} as a decision variable, but it should not be described as a calibrated predictive standard deviation without formal testing.
In practical terms, the safest interpretation is:
\begin{itemize}
    \item the pure-NLL model provides a reasonable first-pass probabilistic forecast, but it would benefit from a heavier-tailed observation model or post-hoc recalibration;
    \item the composite model should be treated as a direction-oriented decision model rather than a trustworthy probabilistic forecaster;
    \item classical volatility models remain a strong benchmark and should not be omitted when assessing uncertainty quality.
\end{itemize}

Future work should go beyond the present audit in three directions.
First, the LSTM could be retrained directly under a Student-$t$ or other heavy-tailed likelihood.
Second, modern post-hoc calibration schemes for regression uncertainty \citep{kuleshov2018accurate} could be applied to the exported forecasts.
Third, if position sizing is the target application, then utility should be evaluated jointly with calibration so that the trade-off between decision performance and probabilistic correctness is made explicit.
